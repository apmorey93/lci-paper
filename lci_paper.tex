% ================= Abstract =================
\noindent\textbf{Abstract.}\;\;We formalize an estimable measurement object---the \emph{Locational Cost of Intelligence} (LCI)---as the value of a cost-minimization program delivering one task--equivalent unit of AI output at targeted QoS thresholds with violation probability $\varepsilon$. QoS enters through convex surrogates for a joint chance constraint. We show LCI is the ratio of operating cost to quality-adjusted throughput, $\LCI=C/(T\,\phi)$, which \emph{declines with utilization} (fixed cost spread over throughput); the latency chance constraint caps feasible utilization, so the cost-minimizing operating point $u^\ast$ sits at the \emph{latency-feasibility frontier}. Serving is modeled using a GI/G/$k$ processor-sharing queue with micro-batching, implemented as a discrete-event simulator that we \emph{validate against the closed-form M/M/$k$ Erlang-C} mean wait (worst-case error $<5\%$). We then estimate LCI for three task families (QA, code, reasoning) over 2023--2025 by combining simulated latency/throughput with \emph{public, cited} prices and accuracies---cloud-GPU list prices, EIA energy tariffs, and published Llama-2/3/3.1-70B benchmark scores (decode rates, token counts, and reliability/safety are documented assumptions)---and chain them into a Fisher \emph{Intelligence Price Deflator} (IPD). In an \emph{illustrative single-configuration} estimation (one open 70B model family, three task families, four quarters, one region), the IPD falls from 100 (2023Q3) to $\approx$46 (2025Q2); a sensitivity analysis varying the assumed decode rates ($\pm$50\%), token counts, latency softness, and QoS weights leaves a substantial decline intact (final-period IPD in $[43,49]$ across all QoS-feasible perturbations; sufficiently adverse latency assumptions instead render the early period QoS-infeasible rather than reversing the decline), so we read the result as a robust \emph{qualitative} fall in the quality-adjusted cost of intelligence rather than a precise economy-wide estimate. The wedge between market price (public API list prices) and the LCI cost frontier compresses from $\sim$36--53$\times$ to below $1\times$ by 2024Q3 as hosted commodity tiers emerge. \emph{Scope note:} latency and throughput are simulated (the simulator is validated, not run on live GPUs); prices and benchmark accuracies are sourced; decode rates, token counts, and reliability/safety are documented assumptions. All numbers reproduce from public data via the released code.

% ================= 1. Introduction =================
\section{Introduction and Motivation}
Economic measurement should price what firms purchase and deploy: \emph{usable, quality-guaranteed completions}, not raw tokens or GPU-hours. We define the \emph{Locational Cost of Intelligence} (LCI) as the minimum cost, conditional on location-specific factor prices, to produce one task--equivalent unit of AI output at pre-specified QoS thresholds. LCI is task-family specific, QoS-constrained, and location-aware.

\paragraph{Positioning.}
Hedonic methods price quality-adjusted IT capital \citep{Triplett1989,Pakes2003}, and the deflator tradition extends this to computing and the information economy \citep{Nordhaus2007,JorgensonStiroh2000}. LCI extends this approach to AI \emph{services} by integrating accuracy, latency, reliability, and safety into a single supply-side cost object. The resulting index is designed for aggregation and macro analysis, and speaks to the AI/productivity-measurement debate \citep{Brynjolfsson2019,Acemoglu2018,Syverson2017,Byrne2016}.

\paragraph{Relation to existing cost measures.}
LCI is distinct from the two nearest measurement traditions. Systems benchmarks
such as MLPerf Inference \citep{Reddi2020} report throughput and latency (and, in
derivatives, dollar-per-query) for a fixed operating point, but do not (i) impose
a \emph{joint} quality-of-service feasibility constraint that pins the operating
utilization, (ii) convert a configuration into a quality-adjusted cost-per-usable-task,
or (iii) aggregate across tasks and time into a superlative price index. Hedonic
price indices for computing and cloud capital \citep{Triplett1989,Pakes2003}
quality-adjust the price of \emph{hardware/instances}, not of delivered, QoS-guaranteed
task output. LCI's marginal contribution is to combine (a) a chance-constrained QoS
aggregator, (b) a queueing-derived latency-feasibility frontier that determines the
cost-minimizing operating point, and (c) superlative (Fisher) aggregation into a
deflator---turning engineering serving data into an economic price object.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
LCI is a \emph{cost} (supply) object, not a market \emph{price}. Its derivatives with respect to input prices recover optimal input demands (Shephard's lemma). Equilibrium prices may differ; LCI isolates the technological cost frontier relevant for productivity accounting.
\par\smallskip\textit{Dual perspective: the Price of Usable Intelligence (PUI).} Define PUI as the market price per task--equivalent unit sold at the same QoS thresholds. In competitive equilibrium with free entry, PUI weakly exceeds LCI; the wedge $\text{PUI}-\LCI$ reflects markups, congestion premia, risk premia, or scarcity rents (e.g., capacity constraints, IP, or regulatory bottlenecks). Thus, LCI is an observable \emph{floor} for the market price of usable intelligence and a natural input into welfare, incidence, and productivity analyses.

% ================= 2. Definitions =================
\section{Definitions and Measurement Object}
\begin{definition}[Task family]
A \emph{task family} is a set of benchmarked instances that share a production technology and evaluation protocol (e.g., code generation, factual QA, summarization). Each family has canonical benchmarks with well-defined scoring rules.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[One AI task]
One \emph{AI task} is a single benchmark instance completed at QoS thresholds $(\bar a,\bar\ell,\bar q,\bar s)$. A completion counts as one unit of output if and only if it meets or exceeds the accuracy threshold $\bar a$, has latency at or below $\bar\ell$ (p95), satisfies reliability $\bar q$, and passes safety $\bar s$. This normalization makes units commensurable across time, geography, and architecture.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Quality-adjusted output.}
Tokens are intermediate. Output is task--equivalent completions with QoS:
\begin{equation}
\QOU
= T \cdot \phi(a,\ell,q,s),\qquad
\phi(a,\ell,q,s)=\alpha(a)^{\eta_a}\,\lambda(\ell)^{\eta_\ell}\,\rho(q)^{\eta_q}\,\sigma(s)^{\eta_s}.
\label{eq:phi}
\end{equation}
Each sub-function is \emph{threshold-normalized} to $(0,1]$, equal to $1$ when its metric meets or exceeds target and below $1$ otherwise, so $\phi=1$ exactly when all QoS targets are met:
\begin{align}
\alpha(a) &= \min\!\Big(1,\tfrac{a}{\bar a}\Big), &
\rho(q) &= \min\!\Big(1,\tfrac{1-\bar q}{1-q}\Big), &
\sigma(s) &= \min\!\Big(1,\tfrac{1-\bar s}{1-s}\Big),\\
\lambda(\ell)
&= \frac{\bar\ell}{\bar\ell+\mathrm{sp}_\tau(\ell-\bar\ell)},&
\mathrm{sp}_\tau(z)&=\tau\ln\!\big(1+e^{z/\tau}\big),\ \tau>0. &&
\end{align}
$\alpha$ is the accuracy attainment ratio (capped at target); $\rho,\sigma$ are reliability/safety attainment as the ratio of allowed to actual shortfall; $\lambda$ is a smooth latency hinge (no penalty at or below $\bar\ell$, continuous penalty above; Figure~\ref{fig:qoslatency}). This is exactly the aggregator implemented in the released code; the multiplicative form makes a near-total failure on any one dimension dominate (strong complementarity).

% ================= 3. Serving Model =================
\section{Serving Model Class and Latency Mapping}
\paragraph{Throughput and utilization.}
Peak (saturated) throughput is set by provisioned inputs; realized good throughput is that capacity scaled by utilization:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:throughput}
T(u) = \underbrace{A_0\,H^{\beta_H} P^{\beta_P} W^{\beta_W} N^{\beta_N} O^{\beta_O}}_{\text{peak capacity }\bar T}\cdot u,
\qquad u\in[0,u_{\max}],
\end{equation}
so realized throughput \emph{increases} with $u$ (more load served per unit time) up to the practical saturation $u_{\max}$. The congestion that accompanies high load is carried by the latency map below (and hence by $\phi$), not by throughput; this is what the validated simulator measures.

\paragraph{Queueing model class.}
Serving follows GI/G/$k$ with processor sharing (PS) and micro-batching of target size $B\ge1$ under admission timeout $\tau_b$. Let $W_\alpha$ denote the $\alpha$-quantile of response time. We map utilization to latency via $\ell = W_{0.95}(u;B,\tau_b)$ using heavy-traffic approximations valid for many-server regimes \citep{HalfinWhitt1981,Whitt2002,HarcholBalter2013}; tail-latency amplification at scale is well documented in production systems \citep{DeanBarroso2013,Gunther2010}.

\paragraph{Cost identity and the operating point.}
With operating cost per unit time $C=\sum_j p_j x_j$ and quality-adjusted throughput $\QOU=T(u)\,\phi(u)$, the cost per usable task at utilization $u$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lci-identity}
\LCI(u)=\frac{C}{T(u)\,\phi(u)}=\underbrace{\frac{C}{T(u)}}_{\text{raw \$/task}}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{1}{\phi(u)}}_{\text{quality markup}}.
\end{equation}
The raw cost $C/T(u)$ \emph{decreases} in $u$ (a fixed resource bill is spread over more throughput), while the quality markup $1/\phi(u)$ \emph{increases} in $u$ (p95 latency rises near saturation, so $\lambda(\ell)$ and hence $\phi$ fall). Over the full utilization range the product is U-shaped; however, the latency chance constraint $\PP(\ell\le\bar\ell)\ge 1-\varepsilon$ binds before the quality-driven upturn, so within the \emph{feasible} region $\LCI(u)$ is decreasing and the cost-minimizing feasible operating point $u^\ast$ sits at the latency-feasibility frontier (empirically $u^\ast\in[0.84,0.90]$; Section~\ref{sec:results}). Beyond $u^\ast$ the QoS constraint is violated (the infeasible region). Figure~\ref{fig:convex} shows the simulated relationship; the curve is \emph{measured} from the validated discrete-event simulator, not posited.

\paragraph{Accuracy from scale and retrieval.}
Neural scaling laws \citep{Hoffmann2022,Sevilla2022} and retrieval augmentation \citep{Lewis2020,Karpukhin2020,IzacardGrave2021} motivate a saturating reduced form for accuracy:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:acc}
a
= 1-\exp\!\Big[-\big(\theta_m m^{\zeta_m}+\theta_R R^{\zeta_R}+\theta_{mR} m^{\zeta_m}R^{\zeta_R}+\theta_Z Z\big)\Big],
\end{equation}
with $(m,R,Z)$ denoting model scale, retrieval depth, and tooling. Parameters are estimable from benchmark panels.

% ================= 4. LCI Program =================
\section{LCI with Tractable Chance Constraints}
\paragraph{Cost-minimization program.}
Let $x=(H,P,W,N,O,\dots)$ denote inputs with location-specific prices $p=(\kappa_K,c_E,w,c_N,\dots)$ at location $\elloc$. For target output $Q$ tasks at QoS thresholds $(\bar a,\bar\ell,\bar q,\bar s)$ with violation probability $\varepsilon\in(0,1)$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:program}
\begin{aligned}
\min_{x,u,m,R,Z,B,\tau_b}\; & C(x;p,\elloc)\\
\text{s.t.}\quad & \EE[\QOU(x,u,m,R,Z)] \;\ge\; Q,\\
& \PP\!\big(a\ge \bar a,\, \ell\le \bar \ell,\, q\ge \bar q,\, s\ge \bar s\big)\;\ge\; 1-\varepsilon.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Define $\LCI_{\mathcal T}(Q,\bar a,\bar\ell,\bar q,\bar s; p,\elloc,\varepsilon)=c_{\mathcal T}(Q;p,\elloc,\varepsilon)/Q$, where $c_{\mathcal T}$ is the value of \eqref{eq:program}. By Shephard's lemma, $\partial \LCI/\partial p_j = x_j^*/Q$.

\paragraph{Convex surrogates for tractability.}
We enforce the joint QoS constraint via either of the following convex relaxations (both conservative and polynomial-time solvable):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Bonferroni decomposition.} Choose $(\varepsilon_a,\varepsilon_\ell,\varepsilon_q,\varepsilon_s)$ with $\sum\varepsilon_i\le\varepsilon$ and impose marginal constraints $\PP(a<\bar a)\le\varepsilon_a$, $\PP(\ell>\bar\ell)\le\varepsilon_\ell$, $\PP(q<\bar q)\le\varepsilon_q$, $\PP(s<\bar s)\le\varepsilon_s$.
  \item \textbf{CVaR approximation.} Define a nonnegative loss $L$ that aggregates economic penalties for QoS violations and enforce $\mathrm{CVaR}_{\varepsilon}(L)\le 0$. This yields tighter feasible sets when moment information is available.
\end{itemize}

% ================= 5. Properties =================
\section{Properties and Testable Implications}
The following are \emph{observations} motivated by the program and by heavy-traffic
queueing theory, with testable empirical content; we state assumptions informally
rather than as fully proved theorems, and verify the load-bearing one (Observation~C)
numerically on the actual serving system in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\paragraph{Observation A (RAG--scale efficiency).}
At an interior optimum,
\[
\frac{\partial \ln \QOU}{\partial \ln R}\Big/\frac{\partial \ln C}{\partial \ln R}
\;\ge\;
\frac{\partial \ln \QOU}{\partial \ln m}\Big/\frac{\partial \ln C}{\partial \ln m}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\text{RAG-dominant region.}
\]
Under \eqref{eq:acc}, the tipping set is characterized by $\tfrac{\theta_R\zeta_R R^{\zeta_R}}{MC_R/R}\gtrless \tfrac{\theta_m\zeta_m m^{\zeta_m}}{MC_m/m}$.

\paragraph{Observation B (Two-margin transmission feasibility).}
Transmission investment lowers LCI through (i) the energy margin (lower $c_E$ at relocated sites) and (ii) the QoS margin (reduced network distance enabling relocation while meeting $\bar\ell$). The optimal site minimizes LCI over locations subject to latency \emph{feasibility}; if network RTT exceeds $\bar\ell$, relocation is infeasible regardless of energy prices. (We illustrate this margin conceptually in Figure~\ref{fig:frontier}; the empirics in Section~\ref{sec:results} fix a single region, so cross-location estimation is left to future work.)

\paragraph{Observation C (Convexity near saturation).}
In many-server heavy traffic, high-quantile response times rise convexly as load approaches capacity (Appendix~\ref{app:queue}); since $\partial\phi/\partial\ell<0$ for $\ell>\bar\ell$, the quality markup $1/\phi(u)$ and hence $\LCI(u)$ are convex near saturation over the full utilization range. The closed-form heavy-traffic argument is exact for M/M/$k$ (the case our Erlang-C validation covers); for the actual GI/G/$k$--PS-with-batching system we do not prove convexity but \emph{confirm it numerically} from the validated simulator---the measured $W_{0.95}(u)$ is monotone and convex in $u$ across the grid (Section~\ref{sec:results}; \texttt{results/tables/norm\_curve.csv}). The economically relevant consequence used here is weaker: within the QoS-feasible region $\LCI(u)$ is decreasing, so $u^\ast$ is the feasibility frontier.

\section{Aggregation: The Intelligence Price Deflator (IPD)}
Let $\{\mathcal T_k\}$ denote task families with expenditure shares $s_{k,t}$ at time $t$. We aggregate using a chain Fisher index---a superlative index that is exact for a flexible aggregator and lies between its Laspeyres and Paasche bounds \citep{Diewert1976,Feenstra1994}:
\begin{equation}
\IPD_t
= \prod_{\tau=1}^{t}\sqrt{
\sum_k s_{k,\tau-1}\,\frac{\LCI_{k,\tau}}{\LCI_{k,\tau-1}}
\cdot
\sum_k s_{k,\tau}\,\frac{\LCI_{k,\tau}}{\LCI_{k,\tau-1}}
}.
\end{equation}
Paasche/Laspeyres bounds follow. Decompositions separate within-family LCI changes, between-family share shifts, and entry/exit.

\section{Empirical Results}\label{sec:results}
We instantiate the framework with a validated serving simulator and public,
cited input data. \emph{What is measured vs.\ modeled:} latency and throughput
come from a discrete-event simulation of the GI/G/$k$--PS+batching station
(not from serving a live model on hardware); accuracy and prices are taken from
published sources. Every figure and number below reproduces from the released
code (\texttt{make results}).

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
The simulator (\texttt{src/queueing\_ps\_batch.py}) models $k$ servers with
server-side dynamic batching (max batch $B$, size-triggered). Per-request service
time is set as $E[S]=\text{out\_tokens}/\text{decode\_rate}$; because latency
scales linearly with $E[S]$ and throughput inversely, a single normalized sweep
rescales exactly to every (date, GPU, family). Defaults: $k{=}4$, $B{=}16$,
Poisson arrivals, near-deterministic service (squared CoV $0.3$, reflecting
low decode-time variance for a fixed output length; the M/M/$k$ validation uses
exponential service separately), QoS weights
$(\eta_a,\eta_\ell,\eta_q,\eta_s)=(0.5,0.2,0.2,0.1)$, softness $\tau{=}2000$\,ms,
full-completion p95 latency SLOs $\bar\ell\in\{18,24,36\}$\,s for QA/Reasoning/Code,
violation budget $\varepsilon{=}0.05$. Decode rates, per-task token counts, and
operational $q{=}0.999$, $s{=}0.98$ are documented assumptions (not sourced).
\emph{Serving topology.} A 70B model in FP16 ($\approx$140\,GB) exceeds a single
A100/H100, so each replica is a tensor-parallel shard across the GPUs of one
instance; we cost the full instance (per-GPU price $\times$ GPUs) and treat the
per-GPU decode rate as the shard's per-request rate, with $k$ indexing concurrent
replicas. Inputs: AWS p4d/p5 on-demand list prices (A100 \& H100,
incl.\ the June-2025 H100 price cut), EIA industrial energy tariffs, and
published Llama-2/3/3.1-70B scores (MMLU/HumanEval/GSM8K) mapped to the QA/code/
reasoning families. Energy is held at a single sourced 2024 us-east-1 industrial
rate across all periods and contributes $\approx$1\% of per-task cost, so it does
not drive the index. The A100 is the cost-minimizing GPU in all twelve cells, so
the cross-period decline is driven by A100 on-demand price reductions, (assumed)
faster decoding, and benchmark-accuracy gains. Sources and access dates are in
\texttt{data/external/} and \texttt{data/evals/}.

$u\in\{0.5,0.7,0.85,0.95\}$ with worst-case relative error $4.8\%$
(Figure~\ref{fig:validation}; \texttt{results/tables/queue\_validation.csv}).

\paragraph{The market wedge.}
Comparing the LCI frontier with public API list prices (PUI), the wedge
$\text{PUI}/\LCI$ falls from $\sim$36--53$\times$ in 2023 (GPT-4-era pricing)
to below $1\times$ in 2024Q3, when the cheapest public API tier (PUI) switches to
the GPT-4o-mini commodity tier (Figure~\ref{fig:wedge}). A wedge below one means
hosted commodity tiers (e.g., GPT-4o-mini) are priced below our self-hosted
Llama-70B frontier---reflecting smaller/cheaper served models, provider scale
economies, or strategic pricing not captured by the 70B self-host configuration.
This underscores that LCI is configuration-specific: it is a floor \emph{given}
the chosen model and serving plan, not an absolute lower bound across all
providers.

\paragraph{Decomposition of the index.}
The four-quarter panel deliberately separates quality- and cost-driven movements.
Through 2024Q3 the decline is driven jointly by benchmark-accuracy gains (rising
$\phi$ via $\alpha$) and falling factor prices. The final segment (2024Q3 to
2025Q2, IPD $71.1\!\to\!45.8$, about a third of the total fall) holds model
quality \emph{fixed}---the same Llama-3.1-70B weights and benchmark scores in
both periods---so it isolates a cost-side move: the sourced $\approx$33\% A100
on-demand price reduction (\$4.10$\to$\$2.745/GPU-hr) and an assumed decode
speed-up. We therefore read the headline as a quality-and-cost decline through
mid-2024 and a cost-only decline thereafter, not a continuous quality gain.

\paragraph{Robustness and uncertainty.}
Because latency, throughput, and hence $\phi$ flow from the assumed decode rates
and token counts, we stress-test the index two ways (\texttt{src/sensitivity.py};
Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}). First, a parametric bootstrap over the simulator's
p95 replication noise gives a 90\% band on the final-period IPD of $[41.8,\,45.9]$;
this band is a statement about \emph{simulation/numerical} precision (and discrete
shifts in the selected $u^\ast$), not about economic uncertainty, and its narrowness
should be read as a non-result. The economically meaningful uncertainty is structural:
one-at-a-time perturbation of the load-bearing \emph{assumptions} (decode rate
$\pm$50\%, token counts $\pm$25\%, softness $\tau\in\{1,4\}$\,s, accuracy-/latency-heavy/equal
QoS weights) leaves the final-period IPD in $[43.3,\,49.2]$ across all scenarios for
which the index is well defined---a robust 51--57\% decline. Notably, the most
adverse assumptions (a large decode cut, longer outputs, or their joint move, which
inflate the per-request service time by up to $2.5\times$) do not \emph{reverse} the
decline; instead they push the slow 2023 baseline against the latency-feasibility
frontier so that no utilization meets the SLO and the index is undefined that period.
The qualitative conclusion---a large fall in the quality-adjusted cost of intelligence
over 2023--2025---therefore cannot be assumption-tuned away, though the precise
magnitude is assumption-dependent; this is why we frame the estimate as an
illustrative single-configuration demonstration rather than an economy-wide
measurement. We also note that the cost-side driver is conservative: we use
on-demand list prices, so committed-use or spot pricing would only deepen the
measured decline.

% ================= 8. Limitations =================
\section{Scope and Limitations}
LCI is a supply-side cost frontier. It does not incorporate demand elasticities or market power. QoS weights are policy or application parameters and must be predeclared. Multi-tenancy is costed via capacity reservations. Dependencies among QoS margins are handled conservatively by Bonferroni or CVaR surrogates. Real systems include multi-stage pipelines, heterogeneous pools, and dynamic load balancing; the utilization-to-latency map and the (here assumed) decode rates should be replaced with standardized serving-benchmark telemetry such as MLPerf Inference \citep{Reddi2020}. Finally, LCI as estimated here is a \emph{private} cost; a social variant would internalize the energy and carbon footprint of inference \citep{Masanet2020,Patterson2021}.

% ================= 9. Conclusion =================
\section{Conclusion}
LCI prices what matters operationally: the cost per unit of usable intelligence at stated QoS. With a serving-aware model class, tractable chance constraints, and a clear aggregation procedure (IPD), the framework enables reproducible, location-aware measurement and supports architecture and infrastructure comparisons.
\par\medskip\noindent\textbf{Future research.} Two directions are immediate. First, integrate demand-side behavior (elasticities, pass-through, adoption frictions) to connect LCI to equilibrium PUI and welfare. Second, generalize the measurement object beyond discrete benchmarks to multi-step or continuous AI production processes (e.g., agentic workflows), incorporating inter-stage dependencies and pipeline-level QoS. Identification using natural experiments in energy prices, capacity shocks, or network upgrades can sharpen causal inference on the drivers of $\LCI$.

\section{Tractable Chance Constraints}\label{app:chance}
The joint constraint
\(
\PP(a\ge\bar a,\,\ell\le\bar\ell,\,q\ge\bar q,\,s\ge\bar s)\ge 1-\varepsilon
\)
is generally non-convex. We adopt either:

\paragraph{Bonferroni.}
Pick $(\varepsilon_a,\varepsilon_\ell,\varepsilon_q,\varepsilon_s)$ such that $\sum\varepsilon_i\le\varepsilon$, and enforce the marginal constraints separately. Each constraint is convex under standard distributional assumptions (e.g., affine uncertainty sets or moment bounds).

\paragraph{CVaR.}
Let $L\ge0$ be an economic loss that is zero when all QoS thresholds hold and positive otherwise (e.g., a weighted hinge). Enforce $\mathrm{CVaR}_{\varepsilon}(L)\le 0$. This is convex in decision variables whenever $L$ is convex.

\section{Queueing-Theoretic Basis for Property C}\label{app:queue}
Assume (i) GI/G/$k$ with processor sharing and micro-batching $(B,\tau_b)$; (ii) finite second moments for inter-arrival and service times; (iii) many-server heavy traffic ($k\to\infty$ with $(1-u)\sqrt{k}\to\beta$). In this Halfin--Whitt regime, diffusion approximations imply convexity of high-quantile response times in $u$ on $[u_0,u_{\max})$ for some $u_0<1$. Because $\partial \QOU/\partial \ell<0$ and $\lambda(\ell)$ is smooth and strictly decreasing for $\ell>\bar\ell$, $\LCI(u)$ inherits convexity near saturation. Micro-batching rescales effective service and inflates variability; under bounded moments, convexity of $W_{0.95}(u)$ is preserved.
